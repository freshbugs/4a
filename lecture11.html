Review

Suppose $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix.

Echelon form of $A$ has a pivot in every row
  ⇔  $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ always has at least one solution
  ⇔  the columns of $A$ span $\mathbb{R}^m$.

Echelon form of $A$ has a row of zeros
  ⇔  $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ sometimes has no solution
  ⇔  the columns of $A$ do not span $\mathbb{R}^m$.

Echelon form of $A$ has a pivot in every column
  ⇔  $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has only the trivial solution
  ⇔  the columns of $A$ are linearly independent.

Echelon form of $A$ has a column with no pivot
  ⇔  $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has a non-trivial solution
  ⇔  the columns of $A$ are linearly dependent.

Reduced echelon form of $A$ is $I$
  ⇔  $A$ is invertible.

History

I found this summary of the history of matrices. Determinants were important early on, even before matrix multiplication. They were maybe sort of invented by Kowa Seki in the 1600s. The word "matrix" was first used by James Sylvester in 1850. It is related to the word "mother", because the matrix is a mother and the determinant is her child.

In this chapter, all matrices are square

Determinants are only for square matrices.

Also, I forgot to say last time, "invertible" is only for square matrices.

Two-by-two

$$\operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ c & d \end{array} \right] = ad-bc.$$

Row reduce: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ c & d \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ ac & ad \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ 0 & ad-bc \end{array} \right] $$

I cheated by assuming $a \neq 0$. Nevertheless, the determinant is always $ad-bc$.

Three-by-three

$$ \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} a & b & c \\ d & e & f \\ g & h & i \end{array} \right] = a \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} e & f \\ h & i \end{array} \right] - b \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} d & f \\ g & i \end{array} \right] + c \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} d & e \\ g & h \end{array} \right] $$

There's another way that involves a bunch of diagonals. You can use it if you know it, but only for three-by-three matrices.

Four-by-four

$$ \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} a & b & c & d \\ e & f & h & h \\ i & j & k & l \\ m & n & o & p \end{array} \right] = a \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} f & g & h \\ j & k & l \\ n & o & p \end{array} \right] - b \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} e & g & h \\ i & k & l \\ m & o & p \end{array} \right] \\ + c \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} e & f & h \\ i & j & l \\ m & n & p \end{array} \right] - d \operatorname{det} \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} e & f & g \\ i & j & k \\ m & n & o \end{array} \right] $$

Explanation:

  • The signs alternate: $+$, $-$, $+$, $-$.
  • The matrix entries go across the first row: $a$, $b$, $c$, $d$.
  • Each of these entries gets multiplied by the determinant after you delete the row and column it was in.

It's called cofactor expansion across the first row.

Theorem 1

You can use cofactor expansion across any row or column. and you will get the same answer.

The signs are $$\left[ \begin{array}{cccc} + & - & + & - \\ - & + & - & + \\ + & - & + & - \\ - & + & - & + \end{array} \right]$$

Theorem 2

If $A$ is a triangular matrix then its determinant is the product of the entries on the main diagonal.

Conclusion

What you need to know

Given a square matrix, you can follow a long complicated procedure, and get a number called the determinant.

You should practice computing them until you're confident. You can make up your own and use a computer to check your answer. Watch videos, eg. Khan academy, or Thinkwell.

What are determinants for?

Mainly:

A matrix is invertible ⇔ it has non-zero determinant.

But you already knew how to see if a matrix is invertible by row reduction. I would say row reduction is better for a specific matrix, and determinants are better for a matrix with variables in it.

Later we'll use determinants to calculate eigenvalues.

You might see them again in calculus (eg. the Wronskian, and the change of variables for a multivariable integral).

What do they mean?

Determinants are a bit mysterious. For two-by-two and three-by-three you can get some intuition related to area and volume.
