§5.1 Eigenvectors and eigenvalues

TL;DR  A matrix times an eigenvector just scales by an eigenvalue.

Announcements

Exams will be graded by Friday.

Definition

$$A \mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$$

  • $A$ is an $n \times n$ matrix.
  • $\mathbf{x}$ is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
  • $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$.
  • $\lambda$ is a number.

Then $\mathbf{x}$ is called an eigenvector, and $\lambda$ is called an eigenvalue.

Warning

  • Do not cancel the $\mathbf{x}$s from $A\mathbf{x}=\lambda\mathbf{x}$.
  • Do not solve $[A|\lambda \mathbf{x}]$.
  • Do not row reduce $A$ to find its eigenvalues and eigenvectors.

Motivation

The equation $A \mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$ is very important, but it is hard to explain why until later. Here are some vague examples.

Geometry: eg. "$A \mathbf{x} = 2 \mathbf{x}$" means the transformation stretches $\mathbf{x}$ to double its length, without rotating.

Markov chains: eg. last time we saw $$\begin{bmatrix}.9&.2\\.1&.8 \end{bmatrix}^{1000} \begin{bmatrix}3\\0\end{bmatrix} \approx \begin{bmatrix}2\\1 \end{bmatrix}$$ $\begin{bmatrix}2\\1\end{bmatrix}$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $1$.

Quantum mechanics: an observable is a certain kind of linear transformation. When you observe it, it randomly chooses an eigenvector.

Example 1

$\begin{bmatrix} 3&-2\\1&0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}2\\1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4\\2 \end{bmatrix} = 2\begin{bmatrix}2\\1 \end{bmatrix}$

Example 2

Is $\begin{bmatrix} 6\\-5 \end{bmatrix}$ an eigenvector of $\begin{bmatrix} 1&6\\5&2 \end{bmatrix}$?

Just multiply! $$ \begin{bmatrix} 1&6\\5&2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 6\\-5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -24\\20 \end{bmatrix}$$ So yes it is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $-4$.

Example 3

Is $2$ an eigenvalue of $ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 4 \end{array} \right]$?

ie., is there a non-trivial solution to $ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 4 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{array} \right] = \color{red}{2} \left[ \begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{array} \right]$?

Multiply out:

$1 x_1$ $+$ $(-2) x_2$ $=$ $\color{red}{2} x_1$
$1 x_1$ $+$ $4 x_2$ $=$ $\color{red}{2} x_2$

Rearrange:

$(1 - \color{red}{2}) x_1$ $+$ $(-2) x_2$ $=0$
$1 x_1$ $+$ $(4 - \color{red}{2}) x_2$ $=0$

Solve: $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 - \color{red}{2} & -2 & 0 \\ 1 & 4 - \color{red}{2} & 0 \end{array} \right]$$

The general solution: $$x_2 \left[ \begin{array}{c} -2 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]$$

Check:

$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & -2 \\ 1 & 4 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} -2 \\ 1 \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} -4 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]$ ✓

So yes, $2$ is an eigenvalue.

The set of eigenvectors with eigenvalue $2$ is the same as the null space of $A - 2I$, (except $\mathbf{0}$ is in the null space but is not an eigenvector).

Eigenspace

The eigenspace of $A$ corresponding to $\lambda$ is the null space of $A - \lambda I$.

$$\begin{align} A \mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x} &\Leftrightarrow A \mathbf{x} - \lambda \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \\ &\Leftrightarrow A \mathbf{x} - \lambda I \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \\ &\Leftrightarrow (A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \\ &\Leftrightarrow \mathbf{x} \in \text{Null}(A - \lambda I) \end{align} $$

The eigenspace corresponding to $\lambda$ is the set of all eigenvectors with eigenvalue $\lambda$, and also the zero vector.

Theorem 1

The eigenvalues of a triangular matrix are the entries on its main diagonal.

Theorem 2

Suppose $\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_n$ are eigenvectors, and $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n$ are their eigenvalues. If $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n$ are all different numbers then $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \dots, \mathbf{v}_n\}$ is linearly independent.

eg., if $A \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{u}$ and $A \mathbf{v} = 2 \mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are non-zero then neither of them is a multiple of the other.

If also $A \mathbf{w} = 3 \mathbf{w}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ is non-zero then $\mathbf{w}$ is not a linear combination of $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
