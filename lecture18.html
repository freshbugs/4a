Question 1

Is $\{(1,4,0),(1,4,0),(1,4,0)\}$ a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$?

No. It is just a set of one vector (written three times). One vector cannot span $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Question 1 without the typo

Is $\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\\4\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\4\end{bmatrix} \right\}$ a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$?

Put the vectors as the columns of a matrix, and then take the determinant by cofactor expansion $$ \left| \begin{array}{c} 1&0&0 \\ 4&1&1 \\ 0&0&4 \end{array} \right| = 1 \left| \begin{array}{c} 1&1 \\ 0&4 \end{array} \right| - 0 + 0 = 4.$$ The determinant of this $3 \times 3$ matrix is non-zero, so its columns form a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Answer: Yes.

Or: row reduce the matrix. Non-reduced echelon form is good enough. You get a pivot in every row (so the columns span $\mathbb{R}^3$) and a pivot in every column (so the columns are linearly independent).

Question 2

Find a basis for Nul$\begin{bmatrix}1&-2&4\\2&-4&8\end{bmatrix}$.

$$\left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1&-2&4&0 \\ 2&-4&8&0 \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 1&-2&4&0 \\ 0&0&0&0 \end{array} \right] $$ There is one pivot, and two free variables $x_2,x_3$. The general solution is $$ \begin{align} x_1 &= 2x_2 - 4x_3 \\ x_2 &= 1x_2 + 0x_3 \\ x_3 &= 0x_2 + 1x_3 \end{align} $$ As a vector equation $$ \begin{bmatrix}x_1\\x_2\\x_3\end{bmatrix} = x_2 \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\0 \end{bmatrix} + x_3 \begin{bmatrix}-4\\0\\1 \end{bmatrix} $$ So set of solutions is Span $\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}-4\\0\\1 \end{bmatrix} \right\}$.

Answer: $\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}2\\1\\0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}-4\\0\\1 \end{bmatrix} \right\}$.

Sanity check: $\begin{bmatrix}1&-2&4\\2&-4&8\end{bmatrix}$ times either one of the vectors is zero, so they are in the null space. They are obviously linearly independent.

Question 3

Find a basis for Col$\begin{bmatrix}1&-2&4\\2&-4&8\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}$.

The second column is two times the first, so we can delete it, and the remaining two columns will still span the column space. Those remaining two columns are obviously linearly independent since neither is a multiple of the other. Therefore they form a basis for the column space.

Answer: $ \left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}4\\8\\1\end{bmatrix} \right\} $

Or: row reduce the matrix, notice a pivot in the first and third column, so take the first and third column of the original matrix.

Question 4

Is $\begin{bmatrix}7\\8\\9\end{bmatrix}$ in the span of $\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\\3\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}4\\5\\6\end{bmatrix} \right\}$?

Answer: Yes. $ - \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\\3\end{bmatrix} +2 \begin{bmatrix}4\\5\\6\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}7\\8\\9\end{bmatrix}. $

If you couldn't "guess and check" that equation, you could find it by solving $$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 4 & 7\\ 2 & 5 & 8\\ 3 & 6 & 9 \end{array} \right]. $$

Question 5

Given two bases for $\mathbb{R}^2$ $$ \mathcal{B} = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix} \right\}, \quad \mathcal{C} = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} \right\}, $$ find the change of coordinates matrix from $\mathcal{B}$ to $\mathcal{C}$.

Notice that $\mathcal{B}$ and $\mathcal{C}$ have the same basis vectors but in the opposite order.

$$\begin{align} [\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B} = \begin{bmatrix}a\\b\end{bmatrix} &\iff \mathbf{x} = a\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} + b\begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix} \\&\iff \mathbf{x} = b\begin{bmatrix}3\\4\end{bmatrix} + a\begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix} \\ \\&\iff [\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{C} = \begin{bmatrix}b\\a\end{bmatrix}. \end{align}$$

Notice that $[\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{C}$ is obtained from $[\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B}$ by swapping the two rows. Thus $$[\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{C} = \begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix} [\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B}.$$

Answer: $\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{bmatrix}$.

Or: $$ \begin{align} P [\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B} = [\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{C} &\iff P P_\mathcal{B}^{-1} \mathbf{x} = P_\mathcal{C}^{-1} \mathbf{x} \\ &\Longleftarrow P P_\mathcal{B}^{-1} = P_\mathcal{C}^{-1} \\ &\iff P = P_\mathcal{C}^{-1} P_\mathcal{B} \end{align} $$ So the change of coordinates matrix is $$ P = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}. $$ It's easy to get confused and multiply these matrices in the wrong order, which would give the wrong answer.

Question 6

(a) True.

A set of $3$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ must be linearly dependent because $3 > 2 = \dim \mathbb{R}^2$.

It's okay to assume dim$\mathbb{R}^2 = 2$ here, since the question is testing something else. But just to be safe, note that $\left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix} \right\}$ is a basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$.

(b) True.

Suppose $A$ is a $3 \times 2$ matrix, and its echelon form has two pivots. That means one pivot in each of the two columns. There cannot be any free variables, so $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has only the trivial solution. Thus the only vector in the null space is the zero vector.

It is the $2 \times 1$ zero vector because the null space is the set of $\mathbf{x}$s in $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$. The shapes of those matrices must be: $$ \begin{bmatrix} *&*\\*&*\\*&* \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} *\\* \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\0\\0 \end{bmatrix}. $$

(c) False.

The unit disk around the origin in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a subset but not a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$. For example, $\begin{bmatrix}1/2\\1/2\end{bmatrix}$ is in the unit disk, but $100$ times it is not in the unit disk any more.

Determinants

The exam from 2014 didn't have any questions about determinants, unless you used them for Question 1.

Question: Given that $\det\begin{bmatrix}2&4&8\\3&9&27\\4&16&74\end{bmatrix} = 48$, what is $\det\begin{bmatrix}200&400&800\\3&9&27\\4&16&74\end{bmatrix}$?

Answer: $4800$, because we multiplied the first row by $100$.

Note: $$ \begin{align} \left| \begin{array}{ccc}200&400&800\\3&9&27\\4&16&74\end{array} \right| &= 100\left| \begin{array}{ccc}2&4&8\\3&9&27\\4&16&74\end{array} \right| \\ \left| \begin{array}{ccc}2&4&8\\3&9&27\\4&16&74\end{array} \right| &= \frac{1}{100} \left| \begin{array}{ccc}200&400&800\\3&9&27\\4&16&74\end{array} \right| \end{align} $$

Some fun facts about determinants:

  • $\det(AB) = \det(A) \det(B)$.
  • $\det I = 1$.
  • $\det (A^{-1}) = (\det A)^{-1}$, if $A$ is invertible.
  • $\det (2A) \neq 2 \det A$.
  • $\det (A+B) \neq (\det A) + (\det B)$.

Invertibility

$A^{-1}$ does not exist unless $A$ is square with non-zero determinant.

If it exists, you can compute it.

The invertible matrix theorem is spread over pages 114, 158, (237), 277, and 423.

Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ matrix. The following are equivalent.

  • $A$ is invertible
  • Reduced echelon form of $A$ is $I$
  • Echelon form of $A$ has $n$ pivots
  • $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has only the trivial solution
  • The columns of $A$ are linearly independent
  • $\mathbf{x} \mapsto A \mathbf{x}$ is one-to-one
  • $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ always has at least one solution
  • The columns of $A$ span $\mathbb{R}^n$
  • $\mathbf{x} \mapsto A \mathbf{x}$ is onto $\mathbb{R}^n$
  • $CA = I$ for some $C$
  • $AD = I$ for some $D$
  • $A^T$ is invertible
  • The columns of $A$ form a basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$
  • Col $A = \mathbb{R}^n$
  • dim Col $A = n$
  • rank $A = n$
  • Nul $A = \{ \mathbf{0} \}$
  • dim Nul $A = 0$
