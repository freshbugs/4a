Change of basis

Let $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{B}$ be bases for a vector space $V$. Then $\begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array}$ is the matrix such that:

$[\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{C} = \begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array} [\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B}$

Theorem 15

The columns of $\begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array}$ are $[\mathbf{b}_1]_\mathcal{C}, [\mathbf{b}_2]_\mathcal{C}, \dots$.

Theorem 15½

$\left( \begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array} \right)^{-1} = \begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{B} \leftarrow \mathcal{C} \end{array}$

Change of basis in $\mathbb{R}^n$

Suppose $V = \mathbb{R}^3$ and $\mathcal{E}$ is the standard basis $$ \mathcal{E} = \{ \mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2, \mathbf{e}_3 \} = \left\{ \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix} \right\}.$$ Then:

  • $[\mathbf{v}]_\mathcal{E} = \mathbf{v}$
  • $\begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{E} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array} = P_\mathcal{B}.$
  • $\begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array} = P_\mathcal{C}^{-1} P_\mathcal{B}$.

You can also use row reduction: $$\left[ \begin{array}{c|c} P_\mathcal{C} & P_\mathcal{B} \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{c|c} I & \begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array} \end{array} \right]$$

Clicker question

If $\begin{array}{c} P \\[-1em] \mathcal{C} \leftarrow \mathcal{B} \end{array} = \begin{bmatrix}1&0\\1&1\end{bmatrix}$ and $[\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{B} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$, what is $[\mathbf{x}]_\mathcal{C}$?

  • B $\begin{bmatrix}1\\1\end{bmatrix}$

Digital music (not on the exam)

One second of music on a CD is a vector in $\mathbb{R}^{44100}$.

(Sort of. Double that because it is in stereo. Use integers between $0$ and $65535$ instead of $\mathbb{R}$.)

This approximates a continuous pressure wave function by a discrete sequence of numbers.

WAV files use basically the same format as a CD. (Usually. There are some quirks.)

MP3 files take up less memory. To do this, make a change of basis and delete basis vectors that you cannot hear. The basis vectors are pure tones and the change of basis operation is the Fourier transform.

MP3 also uses Huffman coding, but that's another story.

§4.8 Difference equations (not on the exam)

Like with a CD, approximate a continuous function by a discrete sequence of numbers.

Difference equations are like differential equations, but you do not have to take limits because there is a shortest unit of time. Instead of $$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$ use something like $$y'_k = \text{scalar}(y_{k+1} - y_k).$$ This is a linear transformation.

§4.9: Markov chains (not on the exam)

In a science fiction future I just made up, everybody lives on the Earth and the Moon. Every year, $20\%$ of the people on the Moon move to Earth, and $10\%$ of the people on the Earth move to the Moon. Nobody has children and everybody lives forever.

Start with $3$ billion people on the Earth and nobody on the Moon. How many are on Earth after a thousand years? Guess, to the nearest half billion:

  • A: $0.5$
  • B: $1.0$
  • C: $1.5$
  • D: $2.0$
  • E: $2.5$

After one year: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{c} E \\ M \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} .9 & .2 \\ .1 & .8 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} 3 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] $$

After a thousand years: $$ \left[ \begin{array}{c} E \\ M \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} .9 & .2 \\ .1 & .8 \end{array} \right]^{1000} \left[ \begin{array}{c} 3 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] $$
