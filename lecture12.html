Theorems in §3.1–2

  1. You can calculate determinants by cofactor expansion along any row or column.
  2. You can easily calculate the determinant of a triangular matrix.
  3. You can calculate a determinant using row operations.
  4. A matrix is invertible ⇔ it has non-zero determinant.
  5. $\operatorname{det}A^T = \operatorname{det} A$.
  6. $\operatorname{det}(AB)=(\operatorname{det}A)(\operatorname{det}B)$.

You do not need to know how to prove them, but I will say something about why they are true.

Theorem 1

Use this to compute the determinant by "strategic" cofactor expansion. If possible, choose a row or column with a lot of zeros.

The textbook omits the proof of Theorem 1 "to avoid a lengthy digression", so I will too.

Theorem 2

The determinant of a triangular matrix is the product of the diagonal entries.

This is because of repeated "strategic" cofactor expansions.

Theorem 3

Recall the three kinds of row operations: swap, scale, replace. Here is how they affect the determinant.

  • Swap: multiply det by -1.
  • Scale: multiply det by the scalar.
  • Replace: det does not change!

I will just show you for $2 \times 2$ matrices. (The book then uses the cofactor expansion and mathematical induction to prove it for larger matrices.)

$$ \begin{align} \left| \begin{array}{cc} c & d \\ a & b \end{array} \right| &= cb - da = -(ad-bc) \\ \left| \begin{array}{cc} ka & kb \\ c & d \end{array} \right| &= (ka)d - (kb)c = k(ad-bc) \\ \left| \begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ c+ka & d+kb \end{array} \right| &= a(d+kb) - b(c+ka) = ad-bc \end{align} $$

You can use this to compute the determinant. Echelon form is upper triangular, so its determinant is the product of the entries on the main diagonal.

Example

Let's calculate the determinant of $\left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 3 & 6 & 10 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array} \right]$ by different methods.

Cofactor expansion

$$ \begin{align} \left| \begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 3 & 6 & 10 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array} \right| &= 2 \left| \begin{array}{cc} 6 & 10 \\ 1 & 1 \end{array} \right| - 4 \left| \begin{array}{cc} 3 & 10 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right| + 6 \left| \begin{array}{cc} 3 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right| \\ &= 2(-4) - 4(3) + 6(3) \\ &= -2 \end{align} $$

Strategic cofactor expansion

$$ \begin{align} \left| \begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 3 & 6 & 10 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array} \right| &= 0 \left| \begin{array}{cc} 4 & 6 \\ 6 & 10 \end{array} \right| - 1 \left| \begin{array}{cc} 2 & 6 \\ 3 & 10 \end{array} \right| + 1 \left| \begin{array}{cc} 2 & 4 \\ 3 & 6 \end{array} \right| \\ &= 0(4) - 1(2) + 1(0) \\ &= -2 \end{align} $$

Row operations

$$ \begin{align} \left| \begin{array}{ccc} 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 3 & 6 & 10 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array} \right| &= 2 \left| \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 3 & 6 & 10 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array} \right| \\ &= 2 \left| \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array} \right| \\ &= -2 \left| \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right| \\ &= -2 \end{align} $$

Dodgson condensation

You do not need to know this method, and it does not always work, but I will show it to you anyway.

Theorem 4

A is invertible ⇔ det A ≠ 0

Use row reduction to calculate the determinant of $A$. You get plus-or-minus a product of scalars times the determinant of its echelon form. None of the scalars are zero because you are not allowed to scale a row by zero.

If $A$ is invertible then its echelon form has pivots all down the main diagonal, so its determinant is non-zero. Or you could go all the way to reduced echelon form $I$, which has determinant one.

If $A$ is not invertible then its reduced echelon form has a row of zeros, so its determinant is $0$.

Theorem 5

$\operatorname{det} A^T = \operatorname{det} A$.

This is easy if you believe Theorem 1. Cofactor expansion along a row of $A$ is the same as along a column of $A^T$.

Theorem 6

If $A$ and $B$ are square matrices of the same size then

$\operatorname{det} (AB) = (\operatorname{det} A) (\operatorname{det} B)$.

Clicker questions

True or false:

  • $\det I = 1$.
  • If $A$ is invertible then $\det A^{-1} = 1 / \det{A}$.
  • If $A$ has a row of zeros then $\det A = 0$.
  • If $A$ has two rows the same then $\det A = 0$.
  • If $A$ has two columns the same then $\det A = 0$.
  • $\operatorname{det} (2A) = 2 \operatorname{det} A$.

Answer: all true except the last one.
