Background knowledge: What is a set?

A set is just a bunch of things.

eg. $\{1,2,3\}$ is a set.
$1$ is an element of it, but $5$ is not. $$1 \in \{1,2,3\}.$$ $$5 \not\in \{1,2,3\}.$$ The order does not matter. $$\{1,2,3\} = \{3,2,1\}.$$ Repetition does not matter. $$\{1,2,3\} = \{1,2,3,2,1\}.$$

eg. $\mathbb{R}$ is an infinite set. If you had an (uncountably) infinite amount of time and paper, you could write $$\mathbb{R} = \{1, 0, \pi, \sqrt{2}, -3, \frac{1}{2}, \dots \}.$$

eg. $\mathbb{R}^2$ is an infinite set of vectors. $$\mathbb{R}^2 = \left\{ \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 1 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} \pi \\ -\sqrt{2} \end{array} \right], \dots \right\}$$

Review: The span of the columns

The span of the columns of $A$ is the set of possible $\mathbf{b}$ in "$A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$".

Question: Is $ \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right] \in \operatorname{Span} \left\{ \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 3 \\ 5 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 4 \\ 6 \end{array} \right] \right\}?$

Let $$A = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \\ 5 & 6 \end{array} \right], \quad \mathbf{b} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right].$$

Same question: Is $\mathbf{b}$ in the span of the columns of $A$?

Same question: Is there at least one solution to $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$?

Same question: Is there at least one solution to the augmented matrix $ \left[ A \mid \mathbf{b} \right]$?

$\left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 2 & 1\\ 3 & 4 & 1\\ 5 & 6 & 1\end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 0 & -1\\ 0 & 1 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{array} \right] $

Clicker: so? is it in the span?

For the above $A$, "most" vectors are not in the span of the columns, because of the potential "bad" pivot on the bottom right.

If you had an infinite amount of time, you could do this with all the vectors $\mathbf{b}$ and make a list of all of the ones that are in the span of the columns of $A$. At the end you would have a list like $$ \operatorname{Span} \left\{ \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 3 \\ 5 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 4 \\ 6 \end{array} \right] \right\} = \left\{ \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} 3 \\ 7 \\ 11 \end{array} \right], \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right], \dots \right\} $$

If you also had magical three-dimensional graph paper, you could draw a dot at every $\mathbf{b}$ in the span. In the end, those dots would make a plane through the origin.

Homogeneous systems of linear equations

A system of linear equations is homogeneous if all the numbers to the right of the equals signs are $0$.

eg. the augmented matrix: $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 1 & 0\\ 1 & 1 & 0\\ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right].$$ Written as matrix-times vector: $$\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 1\\ 1 & 1\\ 1 & 1 \end{array} \right] \mathbf{x} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right]. $$ The trivial solution: $$ \mathbf{x} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right]. $$ A non-trivial solution: $$ \mathbf{x} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} -1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]. $$ The general solution: $$ \mathbf{x} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} -x_2 \\ x_2 \end{array} \right] = x_2 \left[ \begin{array}{c} -1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]. $$

Parametric vector form

You can write the solution to a homogeneous system of equations in parametric vector form. It's easiest to explain by an example.

eg. solve $$ \left[ \begin{array}{cccc} 1 & 2 & -1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 4 \end{array} \right] \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}. $$

Luckily it takes only one row operation. $$\left[ \begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & -1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 4 & 0 \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 4 & 0 \end{array} \right]. $$

Solve $$\begin{array}{ccccccc} x_1 & = & -2 & x_2 & + & -3 & x_4 \\ x_2 & = & & x_2 & & & \\ x_3 & = & & & & -4 & x_4 \\ x_4 & = & & & & & x_4 \end{array} $$

As a vector equation $$\left[ \begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{array} \right] = x_2 \left[ \begin{array}{c} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] + x_4 \left[ \begin{array}{c} -3 \\ 0 \\ -4 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]. $$

Check your answer.

Motivation

For a homogeneous system, you can add two solutions to get a new solution. This is an example of the superposition principle.

Non-homogeneous

eg. the same as the previous example, but with non-zero numbers on the right: $$\left[ \begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & -1 & -1 & -1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 4 & 6 \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 0 & 3 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 4 & 6 \end{array} \right]. $$

Solution: $$\begin{array}{ccccccccc} x_1 & = & 5 & + & -2 & x_2 & + & -3 & x_4 \\ x_2 & = & & & & x_2 & & & \\ x_3 & = & 6 & + & & & & -4 & x_4 \\ x_4 & = & & & & & & & x_4 \end{array} $$

As a vector equation: $$\left[ \begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 5 \\ 0 \\ 6 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] + x_2 \left[ \begin{array}{c} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] + x_4 \left[ \begin{array}{c} -3 \\ 0 \\ -4 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]. $$

Check your answer.

Theorem 6

Suppose $\mathbf{p}$ is just one "particular" solution to the nonhomogeneous equation $$A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}.$$

Suppose $\mathbf{h}$ is the general solution to the homogeneous equation $$A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}.$$

Then $\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{h}$ is the general solution to the nonhomogeneous equation.
