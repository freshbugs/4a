§4.1: Vector spaces and subspaces

TL;DR A vector space is a set of vectors with rules to add and scalar multiply that satisfy some axioms. A subspace is a subset that is also a vector space.

Background knowledge: sets and functions

A set is a bunch of things.

The textbook sometimes talks about implicit vs explicit ways to think about sets. I don't think these words have a precise meaning, but they have to do with being able to answer two types of questions.

  • Implicit: Is [blah] in [the set]?
  • Explicit: Give a general expression for the elements of [the set].

A function is like a machine that takes an input and spits out an output.

eg. the cosine button on your scientific calculator.

Your calculator also has buttons like "+" that take two inputs and give an output. That's a function of two variables.

Vector space

The main examples of vector spaces are: $$\mathbb{R}^1, \mathbb{R}^2, \mathbb{R}^3, \dots$$ A vector space consists of:

  • a set of vectors,
  • a special zero vector,
  • addition: "vector plus vector" is a vector,
  • scalar multiplication: "number times vector" is a vector.
  • addition and scalar multiplication should satisfy the ten axioms on page 192.

If I want to tell you a vector space, I have to tell you:

  • What are the vectors?
  • Which one is the zero vector?
  • How do you add?
  • How do you scalar multiply?

Then you should check three important things.

  • if $\mathbf{v} \in V$ then $\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{v}$,
  • if $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in V$ then $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in V$,
  • if $c \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbf{v} \in V$ then $c \mathbf{v} \in V$.
I will not ask you to check or memorize the other seven axioms, but here they are anyway:

  • $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}$
  • $(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) + \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{u} + (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w})$
  • $\mathbf{v} + (-\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$
  • $c(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = c\mathbf{u} + c\mathbf{v}$
  • $(c+d)\mathbf{v} = c\mathbf{v} + d\mathbf{v}$
  • $c(d\mathbf{v}) = (cd)\mathbf{v}$
  • $1\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$

Subspaces

A subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a collection of some (but not necessarily all) of the vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

A subspace $V$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a subset that also satisfies three important things:

  • $\mathbf{0} \in V$,
  • if $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in V$ then $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in V$,
  • if $c \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbf{v} \in V$ then $c \mathbf{v} \in V$.

A subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is also a vector space in its own right, because the other axioms are automatically true.

Theorem 1

Suppose $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix.

The span of the columns of A is a subspace of Rn.

The span of the columns of $A$ is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$.

(Typo corrected later: it's a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$, where $m$ is the number of rows in $A$).

Subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^3$

The biggest possible subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ is just $\mathbb{R}^3$ itself!

The smallest possible subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ is just a dot at the origin. $$V = \left\{ \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] \right\}$$

In between:

  • A straight line through the origin.
  • A flat plane through the origin.

Secret knowledge: these are the only possibilities. A similar story holds for subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Exotic vector spaces

The only vector spaces we need in this course are $\mathbb{R}^n$, and subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Other vector spaces are important in calculus, eg.

  • The set of sequences of real numbers.
  • The set of all functions $\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$.

Summary of §4.1

A vector space is a set of vectors that you can add and scalar multiply.

A subspace is a subset where you can add and scalar multiply, always staying in the subset.

Every subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ "looks like" $\mathbb{R}^m$ ($m \le n$) going through the origin, maybe tilted at some angle.
