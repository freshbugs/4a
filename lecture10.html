§2.2: The inverse of a matrix

TL;DR The inverse of a matrix is like the reciprocal of a number. You can use row reduction to figure out if a matrix has an inverse, and what it is.

Motivation

The reciprocal of the number $5$ is $\frac{1}{5}$, also called $5^{-1}$.

$$5 \times 5^{-1} = 1$$

The inverse of an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is similar:

$$A^{-1}A = I_n, \quad AA^{-1} = I_n$$

You can't divide by $A$, but you can multiply by $A^{-1}$. Be careful: usually $A^{-1}B \neq BA^{-1}$.

Not every matrix has an inverse

The most important thing to remember about inverses is that not every matrix has one.

eg. invertible:

  • $\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{array} \right]$
  • $\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right]$
  • $\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{array} \right]$

eg. not invertible:

  • $\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{array} \right]$
  • $\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{array} \right]$
  • $\left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{array} \right]$

Theorem 4

$$\left[ \begin{array}{cc} a & b \\ c & d \end{array} \right]^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{array} \right]$$ if $ad - bc \neq 0$.

Theorem 5

If $A$ is invertible then $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ always has exactly one solution.

Theorem 6

  • $(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$
  • $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A^{-1}$
  • $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$

(...whenever these are all defined.)

Theorem 7

A matrix is invertible if and only if its reduced echelon form is the identity matrix. If so, you can compute the inverse using row reduction. $$ [A | I] \leadsto [I | A^{-1}]. $$

For example:

$$ \begin{align} \left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 3 & 4 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] &\leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -2 & -3 & 1 \end{array} \right] \\ &\leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & -2 & -3 & 1 \end{array} \right] \\ &\leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 0 & -2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 3/2 & -1/2 \end{array} \right] \end{align} $$ So $$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{array} \right]^{-1} = \left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} -2 & 1 \\ 3/2 & -1/2 \end{array} \right] $$

Check your answer: multiply and see if you get $I$.

Another example:

$$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 & 1 \end{array} \right] $$ $\left[ \begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{array} \right]$ is not invertible, because its reduced echelon form has a row of zeros.

Elementary matrices

If you do a single row operation to $I$, you get an elementary matrix.

If $E$ is an $n \times n$ elementary matrix and $A$ is a $n \times m$ matrix then $EA$ is the same as: do a single row operation to $A$.

The book uses elementary matrices to prove Theorem 7. They are interesting and important, but will not play a big role in this course.
