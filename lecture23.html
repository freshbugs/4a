The story so far

$A$ is an $n \times n$ matrix. If $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$ and $$A \mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$$ then $\mathbf{x}$ is an eigenvector and $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue.

Theorem 1: The eigenvalues of a triangular matrix are the diagonal entries.

Theorem 2: Eigenvectors with different eigenvalues must be linearly independent.

Let's spend some time with this online demo.

The characteristic equation

The characteristic polynomial of $A$ is:

$$\det(A - \lambda I).$$

It is an degree $n$ polynomial in the variable $\lambda$.

The characteristic equation of $A$ is:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0.$$

Theorem 3½

The eigenvalues of $A$ are the solutions to the characteristic equation.

The following are equivalent:

  • $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $A$.
  • $A \mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$ has infinitely many solutions.
  • $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has infinitely many solutions.
  • $(A - \lambda I)$ has an infinite null space.
  • $(A - \lambda I)$ is not invertible.
  • $\det (A - \lambda I) = 0$.
  • $\lambda$ is a solution to the characteristic equation.

Example

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1&2\\3&4 \end{bmatrix}$. The characteristic polynomial is $$ \det \begin{bmatrix} 1-\lambda&2\\3&4-\lambda \end{bmatrix} \ =\ (1 - \lambda)(4 - \lambda) - 6 \ =\ \lambda^2 -5\lambda - 2.$$

Example

$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1&2&3\\0&1&0\\4&5&6 \end{bmatrix}$. The characteristic polynomial is $$\det \begin{bmatrix} 1-\lambda&2&3\\0&1-\lambda&0\\4&5&6-\lambda \end{bmatrix}.$$ Use cofactor expansion along the second row. $$(1 - \lambda) ( (1 - \lambda)(6 - \lambda) - (3)(4) ) = -6 - \lambda + 8 \lambda^2 - \lambda^3$$

Warning

  • Row operations on $A$ would mess up the characteristic polynomial.
  • You could use row operations on $A - \lambda I$ to compute the determinant, but it is probably best to use a cofactor expansion. With $\lambda$s floating around, it is hard to tell if something is a pivot.

[Added later: after doing more examples, it turns out that I prefer to carefully combine row operations and cofactor expansion.]

Review of polynomials

Homework: review or learn some basics about polynomials and how to factor them.

eg., suppose the characteristic equation is $$\lambda^4 - 3\lambda^3 + \lambda^2 + 4 = 0.$$ Guess and check integers that divide $4$.

It turns out $\lambda = 2$ is an eigenvalue. Therefore $\lambda - 2$ is a factor. $$\lambda^4 - 3\lambda^3 + \lambda^2 + 4 = (\lambda - 2)(\lambda^3 - \lambda^2 - \lambda - 2).$$

It turns out $\lambda = 2$ is a root of $\lambda^3 - \lambda^2 - \lambda - 2$, so we can factor out $\lambda - 2$ a second time. $$\lambda^4 - 3\lambda^3 + \lambda^2 + 4 = (\lambda - 2)(\lambda - 2)(\lambda^2 + \lambda + 1).$$

$\lambda ^2 + \lambda + 1$ has no real roots because $1^2 - 4(1)(1) < 0$.

Example 4 (page 279)

The characteristic polynomial of a $6 \times 6$ matrix is $\lambda^6 - 4\lambda^5 - 12\lambda^4$. Find the eigenvalues and their multiplicities.

$$\lambda^6 - 4\lambda^5 - 12\lambda^4 = \lambda^4(\lambda-6)(\lambda+2)$$

The eigenvalues are $0, 6, -2$.

The eigenvalue $0$ has multiplicity four. The other eigenvalues have multiplicity one.
