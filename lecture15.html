§4.3: Linearly independent sets, and bases

TL;DR A basis for something is a set of vectors that is linearly independent and spans it.

Confusing issues in this section

An indexed set of vectors is something like $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \dots, \mathbf{v}_n\}$, where the vectors come with subscripts. I tend to call it a list of vectors. This is to get around the fact that sets are not usually ordered. Don't worry about it.

The book has many examples from calculus, where the vectors are functions. I will continue to focus on vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$. You can blame me when you are confused in Math 4B.

Theorem 4

A set of at least two non-zero vectors is linearly dependent if and only if one of them is a linear combination of the previous ones.

Basis

A basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a set of vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ that is linearly independent and spans $\mathbb{R}^n$.

A basis for the column space of $A$ is a set of vectors in the column space of $A$ that is linearly independent and such that the column space is the span of them.

Same for null space.

Same for any subspace of any vector space, eg. $\{ \cos x, \sin x \}$ is a basis for the set of solutions to $y'' + y = 0$.

Theorem 5 (paraphrased)

Suppose $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix. Remember, the column space is the span of the columns of $A$. It is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$.

If one column is a linear combination of the others then you can delete it to get a $m \times (n - 1)$ matrix that has the same column space.

Theorem 5½

Here is how to find a basis for the null space of $A$.

  1. Row reduce to reduced echelon form $R$.
  2. Write the general solution to the system of equations $[R | \mathbf{0}]$.
  3. Rewrite the solution as $\mathbf{x}$ = a linear combination. (The scalars are the free variables.)
  4. Rewrite the linear combination as span of a set of vectors.
  5. That set of vectors is automatically a basis!

(See comment number 1 on page 203.)

Theorem 6

Here is how to find a basis for the column space of $A$.

  1. Row reduce.
  2. Make a note of which columns have pivots.
  3. Go back to the original matrix $A$.
  4. Take the columns of $A$ that are in the same positions as the pivot columns of echelon form.

Two views of a basis

In the children's story "Goldilocks and the three bears", one chair was too big, one was too small, and one was just right. (Or something like that.)

A basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$ has to span $\mathbb{R}^n$ and be linearly independent.

eg., for $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccccc} 1 & 2 & 32 & 14 & -5 \\ 0 & 3 & 11 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 6 & 22 & 22 & 22 \end{array} \right] $$ the columns span $\mathbb{R}^3$, but are linearly dependent. This is too big. For $$ \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3 \\ 0 & 6 \end{array} \right] $$ the columns are linearly independent but do not span $\mathbb{R}^3$. This is too small. For $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccccc} 1 & 2 & 14 \\ 0 & 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 6 & 22 \end{array} \right] $$ the columns are a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$. This is just right. If you removed a column it would not span any more. If you added a column it would not be linearly independent any more.
