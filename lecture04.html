§1.4. The matrix equation $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$

TL;DR $A\mathbf{x}=\mathbf{b}$ is yet another way to write a system of linear equations. The pivots of $A$ tell you if it has a solution for all $\mathbf{b}$ or only some.

Theorem 1 (review)

Any matrix can be row reduced. The reduced echelon form you get is unique, no matter what sequence of row operations you used.

Theorem 2 (review)

Every system of linear equations has zero one or infinity solutions. To tell which, make an augmented matrix, row reduce, and look at the pivots.

Is there a pivot to the right of the line? YES Zero solutions.

Is there a pivot in every column to the left of the line? YES One solution.

Infinitely many solutions.

An $m \times n$ matrix

An $m \times n$ matrix is a rectangular array of numbers with $m$ rows and $n$ columns.

Remember $m \times n$ is "rows by columns".

Matrix times vector

eg. $$ \begin{array}{rcl} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} 5 \\ 6 \end{array} \right] &=& 5 \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 3 \end{array} \right] + 6 \left[ \begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 4 \end{array} \right] \\ &=& \left[ \begin{array}{c} (1 \times 5) + (2 \times 6) \\ (3 \times 5) + (4 \times 6) \end{array} \right] \\ &=& \left[ \begin{array}{c} 17 \\ 39 \end{array} \right] \end{array} $$

The number of columns in the matrix has to be the same as the number of entries in the vector.

eg. $ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} 5 \\ 6 \end{array} \right]$ is undefined.

If $A$ is a $1 \times n$ matrix and $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ then $A \mathbf{x}$ is a one-by-one matrix.

eg. $$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 2 & 3 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} 4 \\ 5 \\ 6 \end{array} \right] = \left[ (1 \times 4) + (2 \times 5) + (3 \times 6) \right]. $$

If $A$ is an $m \times n$ matrix then the $i$th entry of $A \mathbf{x}$ is the $i$th row of $A$ times $x$.

Theorem 3

Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix, let $\mathbf{a}_1, \dots, \mathbf{a}_n$ be its columns, and let $\mathbf{b}$ be a vector in $\mathbb{R}^m$. The following are equivalent:

  • Solve $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.
  • Solve $x_1 \mathbf{a}_1 + \dots + x_n \mathbf{a}_n = \mathbf{b}$.
  • Solve $[\ A\ |\ \mathbf{b}\ ]$.

Theorem 4

Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix. The following are equivalent.

  • For every $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$, there is a solution to $A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.
  • Every $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is a linear combination of the columns of $A$.
  • The columns of $A$ span $\mathbb{R}^m$.
  • Echelon form of $A$ has a pivot in every row.

Theorem 5

Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ matrix, and let $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ be vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and let $c$ be a scalar.

  • $A (\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = A \mathbf{u} + A \mathbf{v}$
  • $A (c \mathbf{v}) = c ( A \mathbf{v} )$.

Multiplication is not commutative. Do not write $\mathbf{v}c$ or $\mathbf{v}A$.

  • C: Sometimes.

Explanation of Theorem 4

A row of zeros in an augmented matrix just means one of the linear equations was redundant.

A row of almost all zeros in an augmented matrix, except a non-zero number to the right of the line, means the system of linear equations was inconsistent.

A row of zeros in the coefficient matrix means the augmented matrix is in danger of a pivot to the right of the line.
