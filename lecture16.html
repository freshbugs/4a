§4.1 Vector (sub)spaces

A subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a subset that is also a vector space.

§4.2: Null space, column space, and linear transformations

A matrix has a null space and a column space. An $m \times n$ matrix gives a linear transformation from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^m$. The column space is the same as the range of this transformation.

§4.3 Linearly independent sets; bases

A basis for a vector space is linearly independent and spans. You can find a basis for the column space and null space of a matrix.

Theorem 7

Given a basis and a vector, there is a unique way to write the vector as a linear combination of the basis vectors.

Example

Let $\mathbf{b}_1 = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \end{array} \right]$, $\mathbf{b}_2 = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 3 \\ 4 \end{array} \right]$, $\mathcal{B} = \{ \mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2 \}$. This is a basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Let $\mathbf{v} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 7 \\ 6 \end{array} \right]$.

Row reduce $$ [ \mathbf{b}_1 \mathbf{b}_2 | \mathbf{v} ] = \left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 3 & 7 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 \end{array} \right] \leadsto \left[ \begin{array}{cc|c} 1 & 0 & -5 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 \end{array} \right] $$ So $$\mathbf{v} = -5 \mathbf{b}_1 + 4 \mathbf{b}_2.$$

The coordinate vector of $\mathbf{v}$ relative to $\mathcal{B}$ is $$[\mathbf{v}]_\mathcal{B} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} -5 \\ 4 \end{array} \right].$$

Change of coordinates matrix

Let $\mathbf{b}_1 = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array} \right]$, $\mathbf{b}_2 = \left[ \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \end{array} \right]$, $\mathcal{B} = \{\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2\}$.

The change of coordinates matrix has columns $\mathbf{b}_1$ and $\mathbf{b}_2$. So $$P_\mathcal{B} = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right].$$ Then

$\mathbf{v} = P_\mathcal{B} [\mathbf{v}]_\mathcal{B}, \quad [\mathbf{v}]_\mathcal{B} = P_\mathcal{B}^{-1} \mathbf{v}$

Theorem 8

If $\mathcal{B} = \{ \mathbf{b}_1, \dots, \mathbf{b}_n \}$ is a basis for a vector space $V$ then $$\mathbf{v} \mapsto [\mathbf{v}]_\mathcal{B}$$ is a one-to-one and onto linear transformation from $V$ onto $\mathbb{R}^n$.

[Edited later: at first I forgot the "onto". A one-to-one and onto function is also called a bijection.]

You can refer to vectors in $V$ by their the coordinate vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

Secret knowledge: $V$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$. This makes precise the idea that, eg., any plane through the origin "looks like" $\mathbb{R}^2$.

Summary

Suppose you are given a vector $\mathbf{v}$ in a vector space with basis $\{ \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \}$.

Two ways to say the same thing:

  • $[\mathbf{v}]_{\{\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \}} = \left[ \begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array} \right]$.
  • $\mathbf{v} = x \mathbf{a} + y \mathbf{b}$.

If you are told $\mathbf{v}$ and asked to compute $[\mathbf{v}]_{\{\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \}}$, then you have to solve $\mathbf{v} = x \mathbf{a} + y \mathbf{b}$ for $x$ and $y$.

If you are told $[\mathbf{v}]_{\{\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \}}$ and asked to compute $\mathbf{v}$, then just substitute $x$ and $y$ into $\mathbf{v} = x \mathbf{a} + y \mathbf{b}$.
